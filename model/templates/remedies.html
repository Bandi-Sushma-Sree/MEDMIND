How to Use This Guide

  • Start with safety: Check for allergies, age restrictions (e.g., no honey under age 1), and medication interactions.
  • Follow evidence level: Each remedy shows an “Evidence” badge to set realistic expectations.
  • Track your response: Note what helps, any side effects, and when symptoms change.
  • Know when to seek care: If symptoms are severe, persistent, or unusual, contact a healthcare professional.

General Principles

Home remedies can complement medical care but do not replace clinical evaluation when indicated. Hydration, rest, balanced diet, and gentle activity often support recovery across conditions.

Respiratory: Colds, Cough, Sore Throat

Category
Honey for Night Cough
Evidence: Strong

1–2 teaspoons of honey before bed may reduce nighttime cough frequency and improve sleep quality in older children and adults.

Safety: No honey under 1 year due to botulism risk.

Warm Saltwater Gargle
Evidence: Moderate

Gargling warm saltwater (½ tsp salt in a cup of warm water) can ease sore throat discomfort by reducing swelling and loosening mucus.

Safety: Avoid swallowing; not for young children who can’t gargle safely.

Steam Inhalation
Evidence: Limited

Gentle warm steam may temporarily relieve nasal congestion and throat dryness.

Safety: Avoid burns; keep hot water out of reach of children.

Digestion: Nausea, Indigestion

Category
Ginger for Nausea
Evidence: Moderate

Ginger tea or standardized capsules can help reduce mild nausea (e.g., motion sickness, postoperative).

Safety: May interact with anticoagulants; consult if on blood thinners.

Peppermint for Indigestion
Evidence: Limited

Peppermint tea or enteric-coated oil may ease functional dyspepsia or IBS-related discomfort in some people.

Safety: May worsen reflux; avoid in known GERD without guidance.

Small, Simple Meals
Evidence: Moderate

Eating smaller, bland meals (e.g., bananas, rice, applesauce, toast) may reduce nausea and indigestion.

Safety: Maintain hydration; seek care if persistent vomiting or dehydration.

Headache & Mild Pain

Category
Hydration & Brief Rest
Evidence: Moderate

Dehydration and eyestrain are common triggers. Water, a short break, and gentle neck/shoulder stretching can help tension-type headaches.

Safety: Seek urgent care for severe, sudden, or neurologic-symptom headaches.

Peppermint Oil (Topical)
Evidence: Moderate

Diluted peppermint oil applied to temples/forehead may relieve tension-type headache in some adults.

Safety: Patch test; avoid eyes; not for young children without guidance.

Tea/Caffeine (Moderate)
Evidence: Limited

A modest amount of caffeine can enhance relief for some tension headaches; avoid late-day use to protect sleep.

Safety: Limit if sensitive to palpitations, anxiety, or reflux.

Skin: Mild Acne & Irritation

Category
Gentle Cleansing
Evidence: Moderate

Twice-daily washing with a mild, non-comedogenic cleanser can reduce oil and surface debris associated with mild acne.

Safety: Avoid harsh scrubs that can worsen irritation.

Diluted Tea Tree Oil
Evidence: Limited

Spot application of properly diluted tea tree oil may help mild inflammatory acne in some users.

Safety: Patch test; avoid undiluted use; discontinue if irritation occurs.

Light, Non‑comedogenic Moisturizer
Evidence: Moderate

Maintaining the skin barrier with a lightweight, non‑comedogenic moisturizer can reduce irritation from over‑cleansing.

Safety: Fragrance‑free preferred for sensitive skin.

Sleep: Trouble Falling Asleep

Category
Sleep Routine & Light
Evidence: Moderate

Consistent bedtime/wake time, minimizing screens pre-bed, and getting morning daylight can improve sleep onset for many people.

Safety: If snoring, apneas, or insomnia persists, seek evaluation.

Chamomile Tea
Evidence: Limited

A warm, caffeine-free chamomile tea before bed is a gentle ritual that may support relaxation.

Safety: Check for ragweed allergy cross-reactivity.

Wind‑Down Journal
Evidence: Limited

Writing a quick to‑do list or gratitude notes can reduce cognitive arousal and make it easier to fall asleep.

Safety: Combine with a consistent routine for best effect.

Important Safety Notes

  • This page is for general information only and not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment.
  • Evidence ratings are simplified; individual responses vary. If symptoms are severe, persistent, or unusual, seek care promptly.
  • Children, pregnant/breastfeeding individuals, and those with chronic conditions or on medications should consult a clinician before trying remedies.

Example reference bodies: World Health Organization, CDC, Cochrane Reviews, and NIH resources. Consult clinicians and reputable guidelines for personalized recommendations.
